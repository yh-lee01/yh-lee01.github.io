Graphical models provide a language for expressing the structure of joint probability distributions — which variables depend on which.

## Bayesian Networks

A **Bayesian network** (directed graphical model) represents a joint distribution as a product of conditionals:

$$
p(x_1, \dots, x_n) = \prod_{i=1}^n p(x_i \mid \text{Pa}(x_i))
$$

where $\text{Pa}(x_i)$ are the parents of $x_i$ in the directed acyclic graph (DAG).

This factorization reduces the number of parameters from exponential to manageable. For example, a chain $x_1 \to x_2 \to x_3$ gives $p(x_1, x_2, x_3) = p(x_1) p(x_2 \mid x_1) p(x_3 \mid x_2)$.

## Hidden Markov Models (HMMs)

An HMM is a temporal Bayesian network with:
- Hidden states $z_t$ following a Markov chain: $p(z_t \mid z_{t-1})$
- Observations $x_t$ emitted from hidden states: $p(x_t \mid z_t)$

Key inference tasks:
- **Filtering**: $p(z_t \mid x_{1:t})$ — **forward algorithm** $O(TK^2)$
- **Smoothing**: $p(z_t \mid x_{1:T})$ — **forward-backward algorithm**
- **Decoding**: $\arg\max_{z_{1:T}} p(z_{1:T} \mid x_{1:T})$ — **Viterbi algorithm**

HMMs are foundational for speech recognition and biological sequence analysis.

## The EM Algorithm

For models with latent variables $\mathbf{z}$, direct MLE over $\theta$ is intractable. The **Expectation-Maximization** algorithm iterates:

**E-step**: compute the posterior over latent variables

$$
q(\mathbf{z}) = p(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x}, \theta^{(t)})
$$

**M-step**: maximize the expected complete-data log-likelihood

$$
\theta^{(t+1)} = \arg\max_\theta \; \mathbb{E}_{q(\mathbf{z})} \left[ \log p(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z} \mid \theta) \right]
$$

EM **monotonically increases** the marginal likelihood $p(\mathbf{x} \mid \theta)$ and converges to a local optimum. It is used to train GMMs, HMMs, and other latent variable models.

## Variational Inference (Preview)

When the E-step is intractable (complex posteriors), **variational inference** approximates $p(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})$ with a simpler distribution $q(\mathbf{z})$ by minimizing:

$$
\text{KL}(q(\mathbf{z}) \| p(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})) = -\text{ELBO} + \log p(\mathbf{x})
$$

Maximizing the **ELBO** (Evidence Lower Bound) is equivalent to minimizing the KL divergence. This idea leads directly to the **VAE** (see Post 15).
