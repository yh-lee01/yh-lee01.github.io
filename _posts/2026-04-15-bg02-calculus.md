Most of machine learning reduces to optimization — minimizing a loss function. Calculus gives us the tools to find and characterize these minima.

## Partial Derivatives and the Gradient

For a scalar function $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$, the **gradient** collects all partial derivatives:

$$
\nabla f(\mathbf{x}) = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} \\ \vdots \\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \end{bmatrix}
$$

The gradient points in the direction of **steepest ascent**. Moving opposite to the gradient decreases $f$ — this is the basis of gradient descent.

## Chain Rule

For composed functions $f = g \circ h$, the chain rule is:

$$
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial g}{\partial h} \cdot \frac{\partial h}{\partial x}
$$

For vector-valued functions, this generalizes to **Jacobian multiplication**, which is exactly what **backpropagation** computes through the computational graph of a neural network.

## The Hessian

The **Hessian** $H \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is the matrix of second-order partial derivatives:

$$
H_{ij} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i \partial x_j}
$$

The Hessian characterizes local curvature:
- $H \succ 0$ (positive definite) at a critical point → **local minimum**
- $H \prec 0$ → **local maximum**
- $H$ indefinite → **saddle point**

## Taylor Expansion

The second-order Taylor expansion around $\mathbf{x}_0$:

$$
f(\mathbf{x}) \approx f(\mathbf{x}_0) + \nabla f(\mathbf{x}_0)^\top (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_0) + \frac{1}{2} (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_0)^\top H(\mathbf{x}_0) (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_0)
$$

This quadratic approximation motivates **Newton's method**: the update $\Delta \mathbf{x} = -H^{-1} \nabla f$ jumps directly to the minimum of the local quadratic model.

## Matrix Calculus Identities

Useful identities that appear frequently in ML derivations:

| Expression | Derivative |
|---|---|
| $\mathbf{a}^\top \mathbf{x}$ | $\mathbf{a}$ |
| $\mathbf{x}^\top A \mathbf{x}$ | $(A + A^\top)\mathbf{x}$ |
| $\|A\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{b}\|^2$ | $2A^\top(A\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{b})$ |
| $\text{tr}(A^\top B)$ | $B$ |
| $\log \det(A)$ | $A^{-\top}$ |
